The Story

Three Sentences

“

In a dying empire of imagination, a child who still feels everything must find the creator who has forgotten how — because the boy is the last memory the old man has of himself.

A boy who doesn’t fit the real world falls into his own imagination.
The imagination is dying because the old man who built it has lost the ability to feel.
The boy has to save the old man.

The Moonchild built the Eternal Carnival because the world he was born into had no room for him. He is Sorkel before the fear. Sorkel is the Moonchild after it wins. That is the arc.

What Makes It Original

The Three Simultaneously Unique Things

01
The Mirror Architect

A fallen creator who built the world the hero is trapped in — who is also an earlier version of the hero himself — who is also the primary antagonist in Season 1. The hero must save his own future self from his own future self.

02
The Parallel Reality

Two worlds running simultaneously — the Carnival inside, Sergio's real creative life outside — each affecting the other without explanation. Never explained. Just felt. The real world scenes never announce themselves as real world scenes.

03
The Melancholy Weapon

A weapon that is specifically melancholy: the deliberate replacement of feeling with logic, color with bronze, wonder with explanation — administered by agents who genuinely believe they are helping. The villain you cannot hate.

Season One · Eight Episodes

The Searching

Eight episodes. The Moonchild arrives pure. He does not know what he is looking for, only that the pull is real. Levels 1–3. Real world: Sergio, age 0–9, Culiacán.

Episode 01
The Searching

Dark water. Wrong stars. A boy washes ashore — no memory, no name, both arms clean. In his hand: one painted eye. Sorkel arrives from a gondola: There you are.

Episode 02
The Hands

The sky flickers — one frame. Hands on clay. Citizens diverge. Those who look versus those who don't. The Moonchild sees the upturned faces and understands there is a ritual he doesn't know yet.

Episode 03
La Fuente

The Moonchild finds the fountain archivist. She has been speaking since 1975 without pause. He is the first to stop and listen. She tells him three things. The third changes everything.

Episode 04
The Ladder

La Escalera appears on the passage between levels. The Moonchild asks all three versions of her the same question. Each answers differently. The bronze spreads another inch.

Episode 05
The Boat

Sorkel sends the gondola without a pilot. The Moonchild must choose: climb in and go deeper, or stay where it's safer. He has never been safe. He gets in.

Episode 06
El Toro-Brujo

The deepest citizen of Level 3 drops a single drop of color onto the advancing bronze of the Moonchild's arm. It holds. For now. He understands for the first time what he is racing against.

Episode 07
La Ciudad

The city herself closing her eyes. The Moonchild reaches her just as bronze touches her eyelids. He holds her face and asks her what she sees. She tells him.

Episode 08
The Astronomer

Sorkel and the Moonchild meet properly for the first time. I am your world. The Moonchild's response is not what Sorkel expected. Level 4 opens. The cost begins.

The Enemy
They don't kill what they touch.
They make it permanent.

The weapon is not violence. It is explanation. Los Que Saben arrive with clipboards and patient understanding. They convert artists into archivists. They turn color into bronze, wonder into logic, feeling into data. They say they are protecting.

They genuinely believe it. That is what makes them so devastating. The villain you cannot hate. The darkness that looks like care.

The World · Citizens

Every Character Already Exists

Every character in Eternal Carnival already exists in Bustamante's work — waiting inside bronze and paint and papier-mâché for someone to ask them what they're thinking.

The Gatekeeper
El Portero

A bronze face split across entry door handles. Governs readiness, not permission. I prefer positioned. First citizen the Moonchild meets. Teaches the law of names.

The Archivist · Since 1975
La Fuente

A wall-fountain speaking continuous Carnival history since 1975. Has never stopped. Knows where Sorkel went wrong. Has been waiting to tell someone who can hear it.

Twin Crescent Moons
Las Lunas Gemelas

Two sisters in a centuries-old disagreement about the same moment. One believes forgetting is mercy. The other believes it is El Vacío's crime. They offer forgetting as a gift.

Three Women, One Ladder
La Escalera

Three versions of the same woman at different points on an infinite ladder. The bottom doesn't know what the top knows. The top cannot reach back down. She connects all 13 levels.

The Carrier
El Cargador

Carries the weight of all things that were foreclosed — every possibility the Carnival never became. Does not complain. Is the most honest citizen in the empire.

The True Exit
La Puerta de Luna

The only real door out of the Carnival. Passage through it makes whatever happened permanent. Does not open until Season 4. She has been waiting since the beginning.

The World · Citizens by Faction

Looking or Not Looking

The Carnival has lived with the Glitches long enough that it has divided into factions. This is one of the deepest fault lines in Carnival society — deeper than the bronze, older than Los Que Saben. When the sky flickers, every citizen makes a choice. Citizens can look if they want. That's what makes it a moral choice rather than a mechanical one.

Never Looks
Sol Ardiente
The Performer · The Warm Danger
When a glitch fires, Sol performs harder. He uses the upturned faces as his audience. His warmth is real. The calculating inventory underneath is also real. He pulls people back from the sky with the sheer force of himself. He has been performing for a very long time. When something reaches back from the other side, his body doesn't know what to do.
Always Looks
La Fuente
The Narrator · The Witness · Since 1975
She narrates in real time. Every change, every moment the bronze spreads or the color comes back. She has been narrating since 1975. When a glitch fires, she narrates that too — mid-sentence, without breaking rhythm. When she sees the Moonchild, her narration surfaces something else: the first time he made something and it looked back at him was not in the studio. It was somewhere else. He was thirteen.
Always Looks
Luna
The Cracked One · Every Time · The Crack Deepens
Every time a glitch fires, her crescent tilts upward. Every time, the crack in her face deepens slightly. She sees the Moonchild fully where others' eyes slide past. I am beginning to remember something I was before I was this. She is the only citizen who can see him completely.
Looks, Then Writes
El Escritor
Blank Pages · The Words That Dissolve
He looks at the glitch. Then he looks at his page. Words appear. They always dissolve before he can write them. He looks anyway. Four centuries of this. The thing you are looking for is behind the thing you are looking at. He cannot write it down. If he could, he'd know what it means.
Never Looks
El Toro-Brujo
The Droplet · Four Hundred Years · Not Yet
In his open hand: a droplet. Four hundred years. It does not fall. His thumb moves slightly when a glitch fires. He cannot break four hundred years of concentration to look. The moment of forgetting. Someone chose to stop feeling something. The bronze came after. When the droplet finally falls, what it lands on will remember what was forgotten. The right receiver hasn't arrived. Not yet.
Never Looks
Sorkel
The Astronomer · Four Centuries · The Deliberate Refusal
He built the Carnival. He has never looked up at a glitch. To look would be to see himself as he was. The astronomer would crack. The creator would surface. The Moonchild sees Sorkel not looking — and sees the deliberateness of it. This is Sorkel's cruelest act in Episode 1. Not the infection. The silence while the boy looks at the sky.
Visual Language

Color is Health.
Bronze is the Enemy.

The entire color philosophy of Eternal Carnival derives from Bustamante's own palette — and from what happens when El Vacío touches it. Color is not decoration. Color is life. Bronze is not a material. Bronze is the thing that happens to living color when the melancholy wins.

The rule is absolute: wherever the bronze has not reached, Bustamante's palette reigns — warm golds, deep teals, burnt oranges, the violet of carnival night. The moment Los Que Saben arrive, color drains. The moment feeling returns, it floods back.

The Color System
Warm Gold
The original light of the Carnival. What color looks like when the creator was still in love with making things.
Deep Teal
The water. The Shore. The uncorrupted depths where the Moonchild arrived. Health at its most alive.
The Bronze
Not gold. Not warm. The color of feeling locked forever in its last position. The weapon wears a color that almost looks like richness — until you look closely.
El Vacío Crimson
The color of the wound before the bronze settles. The moment of conversion — brief, violent, irreversible.

Episode One

The Shore — Pilot Beat Sheet

Season 1 · Levels 1–3 · The Moonchild arrives clean. By dawn, he is marked. The clock has started.

Cold Open
Real World — Culiacán
A House · Morning · Young Sergio
A boy pads down a hallway in bare feet. His grandfather already gone to the Buelna market. Room by room — the low places, the overlooked corners. We never see what he is looking for. We only see the searching. He stops at a basin of water. His own face looks back. Eight years old in the morning light. He considers it. Keeps searching.
Beat 1
The Shore
Dark Water · Wrong Stars · Level Zero
A boy washes ashore — no memory, no name. His arms clean. Both of them. Unmarked. In his hand: something small. Papier-mâché. One painted eye. He stands. Looks at the water. No reflection. He turns. The Carnival is there. He doesn't gasp. He looks at it the way the boy in Culiacán looked into the basin — complete attention.
Beat 2–3
First Law · Glitch 1
The Bronze Advances · The Sky Flickers
No reflection in the water. The Carnival stretches before him — vast, alive. At the margins: the ruins. Bronze advancing. Then the sky flickers — one frame. A studio. Hands on clay. Citizens glance up. Some don't look. The Moonchild doesn't know to look yet. He watches the upturned faces and understands: there is a ritual he doesn't know yet.
Beats 4–5
Sorkel Arrives · Title Card
The Gondola · The Shore · The Name
A gondola out of the dark. Sorkel — star-map coat, one eye open, bronze at the margins of his hands. He sees the boy. Goes still. There you are. They talk on the shore. The Moonchild asks his name first. Sorkel is briefly surprised. I don't have a name. / Not yet. TITLE CARD: ETERNAL CARNIVAL.
Beats 6–10
The Citizens
El Portero · Luna · Sol Ardiente · La Fuente
El Portero and the Name Law: no name, no record — you are here without being here. Luna sees the Moonchild fully where others' eyes slide past. Her crack: I am beginning to remember something I was before I was this. Sol Ardiente performs harder when the next glitch fires. La Fuente mid-narration stops when she sees him: the first time he made something and it looked back at him was not in the studio. It was somewhere else. He was thirteen.
Beats 11–15
The Deep Carnival
El Escritor · El Toro-Brujo · The Anchor Activates
El Escritor: words appear on the blank page when the Moonchild picks it up — fade before they can be written. The thing you are looking for is behind the thing you are looking at. El Toro-Brujo holds the droplet — four hundred years, it does not fall. The right receiver hasn't arrived. Beat 15: the Moonchild holds the anchor near a frozen citizen. The painted eye opens. He sees what was lost. The bronze doesn't crack. But he understands.
Beats 16–18
Glitch 3 · El Espejo
Violent · Mirror Market · The Other Side
Glitch 3 — violent. A woman's voice calling a name. A door slams. Non-lookers turn their backs deliberately. The Moonchild goes to his knees. The Mirror Market: no reflections. A figure on the other side — older, reaching. El Espejo: Two kinds of visitors. Those who come in. Those who look in from outside. / The glitches are the sky showing you. This is the water showing you. Different angles of the same outside.
Beat 19
The Infection
The Crimson Beat · The Irreversible Act
Sorkel's hand on the Moonchild's shoulder. The moment is designed to look like guidance — a steadying gesture. Something that could be read as care. Bronze spreads from Sorkel's hand onto the boy's clean arm. Not all at once. Just a seed. Just enough. Sorkel sees what he's done. Removes his hand. Cannot take it back. His face: something with no name after four centuries of melancholy. Regret is too small a word. Recognition is closer.
Glitch 5 · Coda
The Double Exposure
Slowest Glitch · Simultaneous Worlds · Dawn
Glitch 5 — the slowest. Studio and Carnival in the same space, same air. Not a cut. Simultaneous. The Moonchild looks at the hands. He knows those hands. He doesn't know how. In the gondola, Sorkel dictates: Infection administered. Partial transfer. Subject's purity provides resistance. The seed is present. The clock has started. Then quietly: By the fifth glitch, he will know. In Culiacán, Young Sergio finds the papier-mâché figure. It looks back. Next — Episode Two: The Hands.

On the Collaboration

Bustamante has spent a lifetime building a world in bronze, ceramic, and gold, one that millions have walked through and been transformed by. Marc came to this project as a collector first and a producer second, which is exactly the right order. This collaboration is built on genuine reverence for the work and a shared belief that the best stories are the ones that already exist, waiting for the right vessel. Eternal Carnival is that vessel.

Marc Sternberg is an award-winning creative producer, Fulbright Specialist, and lifelong student of art whose work sits at the intersection of creative vision and strategic execution. With 20+ years in global marketing and 12+ years producing film, television, and documentary content, Marc brings an uncommon ability to translate artistic worlds into compelling, commercially viable storytelling. He grew up immersed in art, spending formative years at the Smithsonian museums in Washington, D.C., and has since visited major institutions across six continents. His mother has served as a docent at the Phoenix Art Museum for over two decades. Art is not a backdrop for Marc. It is the lens through which he understands the world.

In 2024, Marc walked into Sergio Bustamante's gallery in Tlaquepaque, Guadalajara, Jalisco, Mexico, and has not been the same since. He found himself standing inside a universe already fully formed: bronze figures mid-flight, animals dreaming in metal and resin, a mythology so complete it felt ancient. The concept for Eternal Carnival took hold that day and has not let go.

As Founder of Son of a Botanist Productions, he manages a slate of 10+ properties across film, documentary, and series. His MFA from Chapman University's Dodge College, combined with credentials from Oxford and HEC Paris, reflects a producer who takes craft seriously. For Eternal Carnival, Marc envisions a series that does not just adapt Bustamante's art. It inhabits it, building a world as layered and alive as the sculptures themselves.
